They are about playing a different game than the one you brought to the table. Here is how to approach solo play correctly.

]]> You bought a solo board game because you wanted to play something on a Tuesday night when your friends were busy. Instead, you are staring at a board full of tokens, a deck of cards with instructions that seem to contradict each other, and a scoring track that keeps moving. You are trying to beat the board. The board is not trying to lose to you. The board is trying to make you play a different game than the one you brought to the table.

Solo play is no longer a bolted-on afterthought in modern board gaming. It is one of the medium’s defining mechanisms. University research of over 104,000 titles confirms that solo play has moved from the margins to the center of the hobby. Yet most players approach solo games with the same mindset they use for multiplayer games: find the optimal sequence, optimize your engine, and crush the AI. That approach fails. Solo games are not about beating the board. They are about playing a specific type of game that the board forces on you, and winning requires understanding what that game actually is.

The AI Is Not an Opponent

Multiplayer games are social contracts disguised as rule systems. You negotiate, you bluff, you form temporary alliances, you sacrifice a turn to save a friend, you punish someone who broke the unspoken rules of the table. Solo games remove all of that. There is no one to blame when your plan fails. There is no one to negotiate with when the board presents an unexpected crisis. There is only you and a system that generates difficulty through randomness and deterministic rules.

Consider Ark Nova, which introduced a solo mode that most players treat as a puzzle to be solved. The solo mode generates a scenario deck that dictates the animals available, the habitats you can build, and the scoring targets. Players who approach it as a puzzle try to memorize the scenario deck, optimize their opening moves, and execute the perfect sequence. They fail because the scenario deck is not a puzzle. It is a randomizer that forces adaptation. The winning strategy is not to beat the scenario deck. It is to build a flexible engine that can handle whatever the deck throws at you.

Or take Wingspan, where the solo mode uses a deck of cards that represent a single opponent. Players who treat that deck as an opponent try to outplay it, to find the sequence that beats the card they drew. They fail because the solo deck is not trying to beat them. It is trying to create a specific game state. The winning strategy is to build an engine that works regardless of what the deck produces.

This distinction matters because it changes how you approach every solo game. When you treat the board as an opponent, you play optimally. When you treat the board as a collaborator in a different game, you play adaptively. Optimal play fails when the board presents a scenario you did not prepare for. Adaptive play survives because it is built to handle uncertainty.

The Three Types of Solo Games

Solo games fall into three distinct categories, and each requires a different mindset. Most players try to play all three with the same approach, and that is why they struggle.

Puzzle games present a fixed scenario with a single correct solution. On Mars is a puzzle game. You are given a specific set of objectives, a specific set of resources, and a specific set of constraints. Your job is to find the sequence that completes the objectives. Puzzle games reward memorization, optimization, and pattern recognition. If you enjoy Sudoku, you will enjoy puzzle games.

Adaptation games present a random scenario that changes every play. Ark Nova solo mode is an adaptation game. You are given a random scenario deck that dictates what is available and what you need to score. Your job is to build an engine that can handle whatever the deck produces. Adaptation games reward flexibility, scenario reading, and engine building. If you enjoy chess, you will enjoy adaptation games.

Progression games present a campaign where the board changes permanently over multiple plays. Pandemic Legacy: Season 1 is a progression game. Every play changes the board, adds stickers, tears pages, and alters the rules. Your job is to survive the campaign and understand how the changes interact. Progression games reward long-term planning, emotional investment, and acceptance of loss. If you enjoy a good novel, you will enjoy progression games.

Most players try to play adaptation games like puzzle games. They memorize the scenario deck, optimize the opening moves, and fail when the board presents a scenario they did not prepare for. They try to play progression games like puzzle games. They look for the optimal sequence, ignore the emotional weight of the stickers, and fail when the board forces them to sacrifice something they care about.

How to Play Solo Games Correctly

Understanding the type of solo game you are playing changes everything. Here is how to approach each type.

For puzzle games: Focus on memorization. Learn the scenario. Learn the constraints. Learn the optimal sequence. Practice the same scenario until you can complete it without looking at the rules. Puzzle games are about mastery. If you cannot solve a puzzle game on your third attempt, you are not trying hard enough.

For adaptation games: Focus on flexibility. Build engines that can handle multiple scenarios. Learn to read the scenario deck and adjust your strategy accordingly. Accept that you will fail sometimes. Failure is not a sign that you are playing wrong. It is a sign that the board is doing its job. Adaptation games are about resilience. If you win every time, the game is too easy.

For progression games: Focus on the story. The stickers are not obstacles. They are the story. The torn pages are not damage. They are the narrative. The altered rules are not chaos. They are the evolution of the game. Progression games are about emotional investment. If you are not crying when a character dies, you are not playing it right.

When Solo Games Break Down

Solo games fail when you try to play them like multiplayer games. You cannot negotiate with a deck of cards. You cannot blame a randomizer for bad luck. You cannot form an alliance with a scoring track. When you try to play a solo game like a multiplayer game, you will fail. That is not a reflection on you. It is a reflection on the mismatch between your mindset and the game’s design.

Solo games also fail when you treat them as puzzles that have a single solution. They do not. They are designed to be replayed, to offer new challenges, to evolve. If you treat a solo game as a puzzle, you will solve it once and never play it again. That is not a failure of the game. It is a failure of your expectations.

Finally, solo games fail when you ignore the emotional weight of progression mechanics. Stickers are not decorations. They are the story. Torn pages are not damage. They are the narrative. Altered rules are not chaos. They are the evolution. If you treat a progression game as a puzzle, you will miss the point entirely. That is not a failure of the game. It is a failure of your engagement.

What This Changes About How You Play

Understanding that solo games are not about beating the board changes how you approach every solo game. You stop trying to optimize. You start trying to adapt. You stop looking for the perfect sequence. You start building flexible engines. You stop treating the board as an opponent. You start treating it as a collaborator in a different game.

This mindset shift is what separates players who struggle with solo games from players who love them. It is not about skill. It is about understanding what the game is asking you to do. When you understand that, solo games stop being frustrating and start being rewarding. When you understand that, solo games stop being a compromise and start being a primary way to play.

Solo play is no longer a bolted-on afterthought. It is one of the medium’s defining mechanisms. The research of 104,000 titles confirms it. The growing number of solo-only games confirms it. The growing number of players who prefer solo games confirms it. But none of that matters if you approach solo games with the same mindset you use for multiplayer games. Solo games are not about beating the board. They are about playing a different game than the one you brought to the table. When you understand that, you will never play solo games the same way again.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best solo board game for beginners? Start with On Mars. It is a puzzle game with a fixed scenario, clear objectives, and a single correct solution. If you enjoy Sudoku, you will enjoy On Mars.

How do I know if a solo game is a puzzle game or an adaptation game? Look at the scenario generation. If the scenario is fixed and does not change, it is a puzzle game. If the scenario is random and changes every play, it is an adaptation game. Ark Nova solo mode is an adaptation game. On Mars is a puzzle game.

Why do I fail at solo games even though I win multiplayer games? Because you are trying to play a solo game like a multiplayer game. You cannot negotiate with a deck of cards. You cannot blame a randomizer for bad luck. You cannot form an alliance with a scoring track. Solo games require a different mindset.

Is solo play really a major trend in board gaming? Yes. University research of over 104,000 titles confirms that solo play has moved from the margins to the center of the hobby. The number of solo-only games released every year continues to grow. The number of players who prefer solo games continues to grow. Solo play is no longer a niche. It is a primary way to play.

What should I do when a solo game feels too hard? If it is a puzzle game, practice the same scenario until you can solve it. If it is an adaptation game, accept that failure is part of the game. If it is a progression game, focus on the story, not the score. Solo games are not about winning. They are about playing the game they are asking you to play.

They are about forcing your opponent to take the bad job, and the person who understands that distinction wins consistently.

]]> Worker placement games are not about placing workers. They are about forcing your opponent to take the bad job, and the person who understands that distinction wins consistently.

Most new players walk into a worker placement game expecting a puzzle. They see a board with action spaces, they see a row of pawns, and they assume the goal is to find the most efficient route from start to finish. The puzzle is the distraction. The game is a negotiation fought with pieces that cannot speak.

When you sit down to play a heavy worker placement title, you are not solving a pathfinding problem. You are playing a game of hot potato with the worst available action. Every space on the board has a hierarchy. One player wants the gold mine. Another player needs the grain field. When you place a worker, you are not claiming the action for yourself. You are deciding who gets the action you did not want.

Consider a standard farm setup. You have three farmers. The market space offers a massive bonus if you take it first. The quarry offers a steady two wood per turn. The river offers a single fish that unlocks a boat upgrade. A novice looks at the board and asks where to go first. An experienced player looks at the board and asks which of those three actions their opponents will desperately want, and then places a worker on the space that forces someone else to take the river.

This distinction changes everything about how you read the opening moves. In Splendor, your opening gem grab decides the game before the first turn ends. Splendors opening gem grab decides the game before the first turn ends. The same principle applies here, but with higher stakes. If you grab the premium action, you leave your opponent with the penalty space. The penalty space is not a punishment for bad play. It is a feature. It is the thing that slows down your opponents engine while you build yours.

Lets look at a concrete example from a widely played title, Brass: Birmingham. The board offers a canal space, a railway space, a market space, and a coal mine space. The coal mine generates the raw material needed to build everything else. The market sells the finished goods for profit. The canal moves goods to market. The railway moves goods to the canal. Mathematically, the coal mine is the bottleneck. Every winning game requires coal.

A new player races to the coal mine. They place their first worker there on turn one. They place their second worker there on turn two. They build a canal. They build a railway. They sell goods. They make a profit. They lose.

Why? Because the coal mine is the most valuable action, and it is also the most valuable action to block. When you place a worker on the coal mine, you are not just taking coal. You are saying no to your opponents coal. If your opponent cannot get coal, they cannot build. If they cannot build, they cannot make money. If they cannot make money, they cannot buy your canal tiles. Your victory is not built on your own efficiency. It is built on their inability to play.

This is why worker placement games feel different from deck builders or engine builders. In a deck builder, you are building your own machine. In worker placement, you are sabotaging your opponents machine while building your own. The two processes happen simultaneously, and the sabotage is the more important half.

The opening move in worker placement is never about the action itself. It is about the actions position in the hierarchy of desire. Is the premium action actually premium? Or is it a trap that forces you to overspend? Is the penalty space actually a penalty, or is it a necessary step that unlocks a second worker? Is the blocked space a threat, or is it a signal that your opponent is targeting your main line?

Reading the hierarchy requires a mental checklist that every veteran player runs before placing a single piece. First, identify the bottleneck action. This is the resource that every player needs but cannot all have. Second, identify the premium bonus. This is the action that offers a disproportionate reward for taking it early. Third, identify the penalty space. This is the action that offers a terrible return, usually because it is blocked by other players workers or because it yields almost nothing.

The goal is to place your worker on the bottleneck or the premium bonus, but only if doing so forces your opponent into the penalty space. If placing your worker on the bottleneck leaves your opponent with a functional action, you have not blocked them. You have merely taken a resource they could have gotten elsewhere. Blocking means removing an option they actually need.

Lets test this against a real scenario. You are playing a three-player game of Agricola. The board offers a pasture space, a farm space, a market space, and a family expansion space. The family expansion space is the bottleneck. You cannot build houses without workers. You cannot get workers without expanding your family. The pasture space offers wool. The farm space offers food. The market space offers grain.

A novice player places their first worker on the pasture. They want wool. They plan to weave cloth. They do not realize that their opponent is also planning to weave cloth, and that the pasture will be blocked by turn two. By placing on the pasture, they forced their opponent to take the market. The market offers grain. Grain feeds families. Families build houses. Houses score points. The novices open move actually accelerated their opponents engine by removing a better option.

The correct opening move is to place on the family expansion space, or to block it. If you take the family expansion space yourself, you build your engine faster than anyone else. If you block it, you force your opponent to take the pasture or the market. Both of those actions feed your own engine. You get the wool and the grain they needed, while they sit with a half-built house and no path to points.

This is the core mechanic of worker placement. The game is not about where you go. It is about where you make your opponent go. The spaces you avoid are more important than the spaces you take.

Once you internalize this, the rest of the game becomes simpler. You stop looking at the board as a map of opportunities and start looking at it as a map of constraints. Every worker you place reduces the available actions for everyone else. Your job is to reduce the right actions.

Consider the late game. You have four workers. The board is crowded. Most action spaces are blocked. This is when the blocking principle becomes absolutely critical. You are not looking for the action you need. You are looking for the action your opponent needs that you can block. If your opponent is one worker away from scoring a massive point bonus on the tavern space, and you place your final worker there, you have just deleted their points. You did not score those points yourself. You denied them to someone else. That is how worker placement games are won.

There is a common misconception that worker placement games are about efficiency. Efficiency matters, but only as a secondary concern. Efficiency is the speed at which you convert workers into points. Blocking is the speed at which you convert your opponents workers into wasted turns. Blocking is always faster. Blocking is always more decisive. Blocking is the difference between a close loss and a blowout win.

If you want to improve at worker placement, stop optimizing your own path. Start optimizing your opponents path. Look at the board and ask which action does my opponent need most? Then place your worker on the space that makes that action impossible. You will lose more games when you try to be efficient. You will win more games when you try to be obstructive.

The board is not a puzzle to solve. It is a battlefield to control. The spaces you take are just the front line. The spaces you block are the supply lines. Cut the supply lines, and the game ends before your opponents engine can even start.
