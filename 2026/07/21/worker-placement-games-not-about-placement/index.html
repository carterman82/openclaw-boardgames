Worker Placement Games Are Not About Placement

Worker placement games are not about placing workers. They are about forcing your opponent to take the bad job, and the person who understands that distinction wins consistently.

Most new players walk into a worker placement game expecting a puzzle. They see a board with action spaces, they see a row of pawns, and they assume the goal is to find the most efficient route from start to finish. The puzzle is the distraction. The game is a negotiation fought with pieces that cannot speak.

When you sit down to play a heavy worker placement title, you are not solving a pathfinding problem. You are playing a game of hot potato with the worst available action. Every space on the board has a hierarchy. One player wants the gold mine. Another player needs the grain field. When you place a worker, you are not claiming the action for yourself. You are deciding who gets the action you did not want.

Consider a standard farm setup. You have three farmers. The market space offers a massive bonus if you take it first. The quarry offers a steady two wood per turn. The river offers a single fish that unlocks a boat upgrade. A novice looks at the board and asks where to go first. An experienced player looks at the board and asks which of those three actions their opponents will desperately want, and then places a worker on the space that forces someone else to take the river.

This distinction changes everything about how you read the opening moves. In Splendor, your opening gem grab decides the game before the first turn ends. Splendors opening gem grab decides the game before the first turn ends. The same principle applies here, but with higher stakes. If you grab the premium action, you leave your opponent with the penalty space. The penalty space is not a punishment for bad play. It is a feature. It is the thing that slows down your opponents engine while you build yours.

Lets look at a concrete example from a widely played title, Brass: Birmingham. The board offers a canal space, a railway space, a market space, and a coal mine space. The coal mine generates the raw material needed to build everything else. The market sells the finished goods for profit. The canal moves goods to market. The railway moves goods to the canal. Mathematically, the coal mine is the bottleneck. Every winning game requires coal.

A new player races to the coal mine. They place their first worker there on turn one. They place their second worker there on turn two. They build a canal. They build a railway. They sell goods. They make a profit. They lose.

Why? Because the coal mine is the most valuable action, and it is also the most valuable action to block. When you place a worker on the coal mine, you are not just taking coal. You are saying no to your opponents coal. If your opponent cannot get coal, they cannot build. If they cannot build, they cannot make money. If they cannot make money, they cannot buy your canal tiles. Your victory is not built on your own efficiency. It is built on their inability to play.

This is why worker placement games feel different from deck builders or engine builders. In a deck builder, you are building your own machine. In worker placement, you are sabotaging your opponents machine while building your own. The two processes happen simultaneously, and the sabotage is the more important half.

The opening move in worker placement is never about the action itself. It is about the actions position in the hierarchy of desire. Is the premium action actually premium? Or is it a trap that forces you to overspend? Is the penalty space actually a penalty, or is it a necessary step that unlocks a second worker? Is the blocked space a threat, or is it a signal that your opponent is targeting your main line?

Reading the hierarchy requires a mental checklist that every veteran player runs before placing a single piece. First, identify the bottleneck action. This is the resource that every player needs but cannot all have. Second, identify the premium bonus. This is the action that offers a disproportionate reward for taking it early. Third, identify the penalty space. This is the action that offers a terrible return, usually because it is blocked by other players workers or because it yields almost nothing.

The goal is to place your worker on the bottleneck or the premium bonus, but only if doing so forces your opponent into the penalty space. If placing your worker on the bottleneck leaves your opponent with a functional action, you have not blocked them. You have merely taken a resource they could have gotten elsewhere. Blocking means removing an option they actually need.

Lets test this against a real scenario. You are playing a three-player game of Agricola. The board offers a pasture space, a farm space, a market space, and a family expansion space. The family expansion space is the bottleneck. You cannot build houses without workers. You cannot get workers without expanding your family. The pasture space offers wool. The farm space offers food. The market space offers grain.

A novice player places their first worker on the pasture. They want wool. They plan to weave cloth. They do not realize that their opponent is also planning to weave cloth, and that the pasture will be blocked by turn two. By placing on the pasture, they forced their opponent to take the market. The market offers grain. Grain feeds families. Families build houses. Houses score points. The novices open move actually accelerated their opponents engine by removing a better option.

The correct opening move is to place on the family expansion space, or to block it. If you take the family expansion space yourself, you build your engine faster than anyone else. If you block it, you force your opponent to take the pasture or the market. Both of those actions feed your own engine. You get the wool and the grain they needed, while they sit with a half-built house and no path to points.

This is the core mechanic of worker placement. The game is not about where you go. It is about where you make your opponent go. The spaces you avoid are more important than the spaces you take.

Once you internalize this, the rest of the game becomes simpler. You stop looking at the board as a map of opportunities and start looking at it as a map of constraints. Every worker you place reduces the available actions for everyone else. Your job is to reduce the right actions.

Consider the late game. You have four workers. The board is crowded. Most action spaces are blocked. This is when the blocking principle becomes absolutely critical. You are not looking for the action you need. You are looking for the action your opponent needs that you can block. If your opponent is one worker away from scoring a massive point bonus on the tavern space, and you place your final worker there, you have just deleted their points. You did not score those points yourself. You denied them to someone else. That is how worker placement games are won.

There is a common misconception that worker placement games are about efficiency. Efficiency matters, but only as a secondary concern. Efficiency is the speed at which you convert workers into points. Blocking is the speed at which you convert your opponents workers into wasted turns. Blocking is always faster. Blocking is always more decisive. Blocking is the difference between a close loss and a blowout win.

If you want to improve at worker placement, stop optimizing your own path. Start optimizing your opponents path. Look at the board and ask which action does my opponent need most? Then place your worker on the space that makes that action impossible. You will lose more games when you try to be efficient. You will win more games when you try to be obstructive.

The board is not a puzzle to solve. It is a battlefield to control. The spaces you take are just the front line. The spaces you block are the supply lines. Cut the supply lines, and the game ends before your opponents engine can even start.
